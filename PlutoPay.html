PlutoPay

An app for all your money needs

While I was part of the CareerFoundry specialization course, I had the opportunity to work on an end-to-end user experience project surrounding money management. I was responsible for carrying out the project throughout the entire UX process, including phases of inspiration, conceptualization, iteration, and exposition.

Context

Young individuals are having difficulties with efficiently managing their money.

In a rapidly evolving digital landscape, young people often face the challenge of efficiently managing different financial activities with ease and minimal effort.

Problem

I found that young consumers seeking control of their finances and effective cross-border money management are often frustrated when navigating multiple financial platforms for different tasks. The causes of these frustrations include: inefficient processes, limited time, complicated financial jargon, and absence of a holistic financial overview.

Solution

PlutoPay addresses this challenge by providing a consolidated platform where users can seamlessly perform a wide array of financial activities. Through intuitive features for transfers, payments, savings, investments, spending, and budgeting, the app offers a unified financial solution, empowering individuals to take control of their finances effortlessly in one comprehensive app.

Question

How might we help young people manage their money effectively and efficiently amidst the swiftly changing digital landscape and the growing constraints of time?

Discover: User interviews

My interviewees were using at least 2 platforms to manage their finances.

To better understand the problem space and refine my hypothesized solution, I collected qualitative data by leveraging user interviews. By means of the interviews, I had a two-tier objective:

  1. Explore potential users preferences and expectations surrounding money management and online transactions;
  2. Identify potential users needs and pain points when managing their finances.
Main insights

Potential users’ highest values are convenience and efficiency.

Based on the trends in the affinity map, I identified that potential users value convenience and efficiency. It also became clear that our users prefer an app solution for the problem of money management as they are interested in managing money on the go and with little effort.

Theme 1: Centralization

Interviewees want to transfer, receive, pay, budget, monitor spending, and invest in one single app.

Ability to view all bank accounts, across countries, in one app is key.

Potential users want to do everything money-related online. Going to a physical bank is perceived as a source of stress.

Theme 2: Motivation

Interviewees desire financial freedom, but need assistance on the path to their financial goals.

Being aware of one’s money inflow v outflow increases feelings of financial accomplishment.

Surprises are a no-go when it comes to money. Interviewees need to be informed of upcoming payments or changes to their finances.

Theme 3: Time scarcity

Interviewees will almost always pick the payment method that doesn’t require them to enter bank or card details.

Features that are easy to learn are more valuable than intricate features even if the former does not provide as much value.

When making transfers to friends/family that require filling in banking details, inconvenience and frustration is high.

User journeys

The making of PlutoPay

As a first step for designing our app solution, I translated our user goals into journey maps.

This allowed for a north-star visualization of the processes - practical, emotional, and mental - that our different users go through in order to accomplish key goals related to money management.

Importantly, by creating a visual story of these goals, I identified opportunities of development for PlutoPay, which directed my steps forward.

With the user journeys providing a high-level overview of the user experience, I was able to decide which features were most valuable to users, and developed user stories around these findings. Wireframes & Prototypes

Creating lo-fi wireframes and mid-fi rapid prototyping

To ensure a logical organization of user flows, I crafted a site map and refined it through a closed card sort with over 5 participants. Armed with the fine-tuned structure and the core features’ flows, I created low-fidelity wireframes. These were translated into digital wireframes and evolved into a clickable mid-fidelity prototype that was used for usability testing.

Usability testing: Uncovering design gaps

One principle overlooking the rest...

Given that our user research unveiled that convenience and efficiency were top priorities for potential users, I initially centered my design approach purely around simplicity. However, feedback from mentors and from usability testing with 8 peers highlighted that my emphasis on simplicity overlooked additional critical user needs, such as assistance and guidance.

Iterative design & design improvements

3 major improvements: A more holistic approach to simplicity

After continuously iterating on my design in response to user feedback, I came up with 3 major improvements that maintained simplicity as a value while complementing it with assistance and guidance:

Attaching saving goals to spending limits

  • Initially, I considered discipline as the sole motivator for users to spend less.
  • User feedback revealed that supplementing a spending limit with a saving goal would incentivize users to stay within their spending limit.
  • Adding a CTA to create a saving goal after setting a spending limit provides the ‘why’ for the latter.

Adding reminders for achieving goals

  • At first, I was concerned that reminders would reduce the design’s simplicity, driving me to only add a notifications button towards the end of the funnel.
  • Usability testing revealed that users benefit from reminders about spending limits and saving goals.
  • Implementing reminders in the funnel itself ensures users stay focused on their financial objectives.

Creating a questionnaire for investments

  • Originally, I had a 'Recommended for You' section for investments based on spending habits.
  • User feedback emphasized a preference for recommendations derived from personally answered questions rather than an opaque backend algorithm.
  • A questionnaire fosters a sense of control over users’ finances and is more personalized.

Conclusion & Learnings

What I learnt and would do differently next time

Beyond the tangible output of PlutoPay, the experience of applying an entire UX process to a product was highly informative. It has provided me with valuable insights and a clear direction on how to approach future UX projects. That being said, here are a few things I've learned:

  1. Collecting continuous user feedback: While working on PlutoPay, I gathered user insights early on and conducted usability testing post first prototype. Yet, my process can be improved by maintaining a continuous feedback loop throughout development, such as implementing rounds of rapid prototyping, to address user needs upfront and minimize significant adjustments later on.
  2. Embracing iterative progress over perfection: I had the tendency to postpone PlutoPay’s product development in pursuit of a "perfect" solution, resulting in project delays. I have learned though, that instead of striving for perfection from the outset, prioritizing the delivery of a functional and valuable product in a timely manner is key. This allows for iterative progress and continuous refinement based on user feedback and evolving requirements, ultimately leading to better results.
  3. Prioritizing early organization of work files: On a more practical note, I overlooked the organization of my Figma file and layers initially, leading to the need for extensive edits towards the project's end, consuming valuable time. This has taught me the importance of diligently organizing the file and layers right from the beginning in future projects to establish a more seamless workflow.
